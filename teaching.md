  <h1>History as active inquiry.</h1>
  <div class="prose">
    <p>My approach to teaching history is shaped by an international academic trajectory and by a commitment to helping students understand the past as a dynamic and contested process. Having studied and taught in Turkey, France, and the United States, I have worked with students from diverse educational and cultural backgrounds. These experiences have reinforced my belief that effective history teaching combines analytical rigor with accessibility, structure with curiosity, and global perspectives with careful attention to context.</p>
    <p>At the center of my teaching philosophy is the conviction that history is best learned through active engagement with questions, sources, and debates. Rather than asking students simply to absorb information, I encourage them to think historically: to ask how knowledge is produced, whose voices are amplified or silenced, how interpretations change over time, and why evidence matters. In courses on the Middle East, the Ottoman Empire, and the Islamic world, I foreground themes such as reform, war, social change, intellectual authority, empire, religion, and public life while situating the region within broader global and comparative frameworks.</p>
    <p>At the University of Arizona, I have served as Instructor of Record for <em>Introduction to Political Islam</em> and <em>Middle East Ethnic and Religious Minorities</em>, and as a teaching assistant in survey and thematic courses on Middle Eastern history, Islamic civilization, politics, and humanities. These courses have prepared me to teach students with different levels of prior knowledge. Some arrive with substantial background in the region, while others are encountering the Middle East for the first time. In this context, I use structured discussion techniques to promote broad participation and peer learning. One example is a rotating small-group discussion method in which students first work on guiding questions in small groups, then regroup with peers from other groups to share and synthesize ideas. This keeps classroom energy high, builds accountability, and turns preparation into a shared learning process.</p>
    <p>Source-based learning is central to my pedagogy. I regularly incorporate primary sources such as newspapers, political texts, memoirs, visual materials, travel accounts, and historical documents. I guide students through close reading and contextual analysis so that they learn how historical arguments are constructed from incomplete and contested evidence. I also design assignments that connect historical study to contemporary public debates. In my <em>Introduction to Political Islam</em> course, for example, students complete a media analysis project in which they compare how different outlets present political and historical themes related to the Middle East. They analyze authorship, perspective, framing, and bias in relation to course concepts, and may present their findings through essays, podcasts, videos, or digital projects.</p>
    <p>My formal training in college teaching at the University of Arizona has deepened my commitment to learner-centered pedagogy, backward design, and evidence-based teaching practices. I design courses around clear learning objectives and align readings, discussions, assignments, and assessments to those goals. I use educational technologies selectively, not as decoration, but to support collaboration, organization, accessibility, and creative engagement. For me, teaching is a form of research and reflection: I observe what works, gather feedback, adjust my methods, and continually refine the relationship between historical knowledge, classroom practice, and student learning.</p>
