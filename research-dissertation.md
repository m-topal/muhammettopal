    <p>As a historian of the late Ottoman Empire, I study how intellectual authority, public life, and political judgment were made in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. My work challenges two common ways of reading Ottoman intellectuals. The first reduces them to political labels: loyalist, oppositionist, conservative, liberal, Young Turk, Islamist, Ottomanist. The second treats them mainly as transmitters of European ideas, as if Ottoman intellectual life were a delayed version of European modernity. Against these approaches, I ask how Ottoman intellectuals actually lived, worked, wrote, taught, advised, criticized, and moved through the institutions that made their authority possible.</p>

    <p>My dissertation examines this problem through the life and writings of Mehmed Murad Bey (1854 to 1917), a Dagestani émigré who became one of the most complex intellectual figures of the late Ottoman world. Murad was not only a journalist or political actor. He was also a historian, novelist, teacher of history, bureaucrat, memoirist, and public moralist. This multiplicity is what makes him important. His life allows me to study the Ottoman intellectual not as a fixed social type, but as a figure formed at the intersection of migration, education, bureaucracy, historical writing, journalism, and political crisis.</p>

    <p>My dissertation, <em>The Making of an Ottoman Public Intellectual: Mehmed Murad Bey between Friendly Advisor and Critical Opponent</em>, is not a conventional biography. Biography is my method, not simply my object. I use Murad’s life to ask how a late Ottoman public intellectual became possible, how he made sense of the Ottoman world through writing, and how proximity to power eventually turned intellectual labor into political risk. The project is organized around three movements: formation, worldmaking, and political existence.</p>

    <p>In the first part, <strong>Formation</strong>, I reconstruct the conditions that made Murad possible. I begin with Dagestan after the Caucasian War and with Murad’s early education in the Russian imperial school system. This part asks how a Muslim child from a highland society under Russian rule acquired the linguistic, institutional, and intellectual resources that later allowed him to enter the Ottoman center. I describe this process through the concept of peripheral centrality: the paradox by which marginal positions can generate forms of knowledge, mobility, and flexibility that become valuable at the center. The point is not to romanticize marginality. Murad’s peripheral formation gave him resources, but it also produced fragility. He could enter the Ottoman world with unusual speed, but he never fully escaped the vulnerability of being both inside and outside.</p>

    <p>The second part, <strong>Worldmaking</strong>, is the center of my current work. Here I study the moment when Murad turns from being formed by worlds to forming worlds in writing. I examine three genres: history, literature, and journalism. In his historical works, Murad ordered the Ottoman past and placed the empire within universal history. In his fiction, especially <em>Turfanda mı Yoksa Turfa mı?</em> [Fresh or Stale?], he imagined social types and moral possibilities for Ottoman modernity. In <em>Mizan</em>, he turned journalism into a practice of public judgment, teaching readers how to recognize disorder, responsibility, reform, and political failure. These genres were not separate activities. They were three modes of the same intellectual labor: making the Ottoman world legible to readers living through crisis and transformation.</p>

    <p>In the third part, <strong>Political Existence</strong>, I follow the consequences of this position. Murad’s authority depended on proximity to power. He could teach because he entered state institutions. He could advise because he had access to political elites. He could publish because he understood the language of reform from within the Ottoman bureaucratic world. But the same proximity that gave him authority also made criticism dangerous. This part follows Murad’s movement from advice to estrangement, from journalism to exile, from Young Turk leadership to return, and finally to marginalization after the Second Constitutional Revolution. The central question is not whether Murad was loyal or oppositional. It is how late Ottoman intellectual life forced figures like him to move between loyalty and dissent, service and criticism, access and vulnerability.</p>

    <p>Methodologically, the dissertation combines intellectual history, global microhistory, biography, historiography, and historical sociology. I work across Ottoman Turkish, Turkish, French, Russian, Arabic, and English sources, including newspapers, memoirs, autobiographical writings, correspondence, diplomatic reports, archival registers, literary texts, and historical works. I also use spatial and network approaches when they clarify historical problems. The aim is not to replace close reading, but to make visible the relationships among people, places, institutions, genres, and publics.</p>

    <p>Across the dissertation, Murad matters because his life makes visible a larger Ottoman problem: how an intellectual could be formed by imperial structures, make those structures intelligible through writing, and then be endangered by the very public authority he had acquired.</p>
