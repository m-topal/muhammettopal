<section class="section" id="research">
  <div class="section-head">
    <p class="section-label">Research</p>
    <div class="section-copy">
      <h2>The making of intellectual and political life in the late Ottoman world.</h2>
      <p>My research examines the formation of political thought and intellectual life in the late Ottoman Empire through transimperial networks that connected Istanbul, the Caucasus, Paris, Cairo, and other intellectual centers. I combine archival research, intellectual biography, social network analysis, and comparative historical sociology to understand how ideas moved through texts, institutions, personal ties, and bureaucratic practices.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="grid-3">
    <div class="card"><h3>Dissertation</h3><p><em>The Making of an Ottoman Public Intellectual: Mehmed Murad Bey Between Friendly Advisor and Critical Opponent</em> traces how a Dagestani émigré became a historian, journalist, bureaucrat, political exile, and critic.</p></div>
    <div class="card"><h3>Methods</h3><p>Intellectual biography, global microhistory, archival research, print culture, social network analysis, and comparative empire studies.</p></div>
    <div class="card"><h3>Sources</h3><p>Ottoman Turkish, French, Russian, English, Arabic, and Persian materials, including newspapers, correspondence, diplomatic reports, and institutional records.</p></div>
  </div>
</section>

    <div class="section-copy">
      <h2>History as active inquiry.</h2>
      <p>My teaching is shaped by academic experience in Turkey, France, and the United States. I teach history as a dynamic and contested process, asking students to work with questions, sources, and debates rather than absorb a fixed narrative. My courses use primary sources, structured discussion, media analysis, and creative assignments to connect historical study to public life.</p>
